Sprint Timer

Python, YOLO, Librosa, OpenCV, Matplotlib, NumPy

Project Background

As both a student athlete and developer, I recognized the need for accurate sprint timing without requiring multiple people to operate stopwatches. The Sprint Timer project was born from this need - a tool that could automatically detect, track, and time sprinters using only video footage.

The Problem

Traditional sprint timing methods are either:

  • Manual (requiring human operators with stopwatches)
  • Expensive (professional timing systems)
  • Limited (not adaptable to different environments)

These limitations make it difficult for individual athletes, small teams, or schools to get accurate timing data without significant resources.

The Solution

The Sprint Timer uses computer vision and machine learning to:

  • Automatically detect athletes in video footage
  • Track their movement throughout a sprint
  • Identify start and finish events
  • Calculate accurate sprint times

All of this is accomplished using a standard video camera or smartphone footage, making it accessible to anyone.

Development Process

The development involved several key challenges:

  • Training YOLO to reliably detect athletes in various lighting conditions and environments
  • Creating algorithms to accurately determine start and finish moments
  • Developing audio processing to detect starting gun sounds
  • Building a user-friendly interface for analyzing results

Results & Impact

The Sprint Timer achieves timing accuracy within 0.1 seconds of professional systems, making it a viable alternative for training purposes. It has been used by my track team to analyze performance and has potential applications in coaching, athletic development, and sports science research.

Future Development

Ongoing improvements include:

  • Mobile app development for real-time analysis
  • Support for multiple athlete tracking
  • Integration with wearable devices for additional metrics
  • Cloud-based storage and analysis of timing data
